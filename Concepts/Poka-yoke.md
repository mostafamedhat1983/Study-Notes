Poka-yoke means mistake-proofing or error-proofing. It is a Lean approach that designs a process, product, or step so errors are either prevented completely or detected immediately before they become defects.

## Core idea

Instead of depending only on people to “be careful,” poka-yoke changes the system so the correct action is easier and the wrong action is harder or impossible. Lean sources describe it as building quality into the process at the source rather than relying mainly on inspection after the fact.

## Why it matters

Poka-yoke helps reduce defects, rework, waste, and cost, while improving consistency and quality. Because it prevents errors early, it also supports safer and more reliable operations.

## Common forms

A poka-yoke can work by:

- Preventing the mistake from happening at all, such as a connector that only fits one way.
    
- Detecting the mistake immediately, such as a sensor, alert, or forced validation check.
    
- Enforcing the right sequence or quantity, such as requiring all steps to be completed before moving on.
    

## Simple examples

A USB plug shape that only fits one orientation is a familiar everyday example of mistake-proofing. In a work process, a form that refuses submission until required fields are completed is also a poka-yoke because it blocks a common error before it moves forward.

## Lean connection

Poka-yoke is strongly associated with Lean and the Toyota Production System because it supports Lean’s goals of quality at the source, waste reduction, and continuous improvement. Some sources also note it is widely used alongside Six Sigma, but it is most commonly discussed as a Lean mistake-proofing technique.
