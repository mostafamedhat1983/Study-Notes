Kaizen is a core part of Lean, not something separate from it. it is described as continuous improvement within Lean and as a foundational idea in lean manufacturing and the Toyota Way
Lean focuses on delivering customer value while eliminating waste. Kaizen is one of the main ways Lean does that, by driving small, continuous improvements in processes, systems, and daily work.

## Simple relationship

A clear way to think about it is:

- Lean = the broader system or philosophy for reducing waste and improving flow.
    
- Kaizen = the continuous improvement habit inside that system.
    

## Why people connect them

Lean identifies waste and inefficiency, while kaizen gives teams a practical way to keep fixing those problems over time. That is why some sources call kaizen a core concept of Lean and even say lean production is founded on the idea of kaizen.

Kaizen means continuous improvement through small, ongoing changes rather than big one-time fixes. In Lean, it is both a philosophy and a practice: improve processes continuously, reduce waste, involve everyone, and make work better over time.

## Core idea

Kaizen is based on the belief that everything can be improved and that small changes can compound into major results over time. It is closely tied to Lean and the Toyota approach, where the goal is to improve quality, efficiency, flow, and customer value while reducing waste.

## What makes it different

Kaizen is usually incremental, low-risk, and continuous, not a dramatic transformation program. It also emphasizes employee involvement, meaning improvement is not only management’s job; people doing the work are expected to help identify problems and suggest better ways.

## Typical cycle

A common kaizen pattern PDCA is:

-- Plan: identify the problem, analyze causes, define a goal, and decide what change to test.
    
- Do: try the change, usually on a small scale first.
    
- Check: measure the results and compare them with the expected outcome.
    
- Act: keep, standardize, adjust, or replace the change based on what you learned, then repeat the cycle.

## Simple example

If a team notices deployments fail because engineers forget one manual step, a kaizen approach would not wait for a huge transformation project. It would first improve the checklist, automate the step if possible, measure results, and keep refining the process in small cycles.

## In one sentence

Kaizen = “change for the better” through continuous, small improvements involving everyone
